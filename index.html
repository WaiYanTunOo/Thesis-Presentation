The Factorial Dilemma (1720s)

Bridging the Gap

Problem: Factorial $n!$ is defined only for integers.

  • $3! = 6$
  • $4! = 24$

The Question: What is $2.5!$?

Euler's answer: Build a smooth interpolation — the Gamma function.

Euler's Resolution

Euler's Integral

Euler's integral of the second kind:

$$\Gamma(z) = \int_{0}^{\infty} t^{z-1} e^{-t} dt, \quad \Re(z) > 0$$

Recurrence

The key property:

$$\Gamma(z+1) = z\Gamma(z)$$

This ensures $\Gamma(n+1) = n!$.

Analytic Continuation

Properties

$\Gamma(z)$ extends meromorphically to $\mathbb{C}$.

It has simple poles at $0, -1, -2, \dots$

Important special value (Proof in Appendix A):

$$\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$$

The Beta Function

Definition

Euler's integral of the first kind:

$$B(x,y) = \int_{0}^{1} t^{x-1} (1-t)^{y-1} dt$$

Defined for $\Re(x), \Re(y) > 0$.

Symmetry

$$B(x,y) = B(y,x)$$

Ideal for modeling binary outcomes like "Head/Tail" or "Success/Failure".

The Master Identity

The Beta-Gamma Identity

$$B(x,y) = \frac{\Gamma(x)\Gamma(y)}{\Gamma(x+y)}$$

Utility

This identity converts complex double integrals into products of single integrals.

It acts as a computational bridge, essential for Bayesian inference.

Bohr-Mollerup: Why Gamma is unique

Uniqueness (1922)

The unique $f$ satisfying:

  1. $f(1)=1$
  2. $f(x+1)=x f(x)$
  3. $\log f(x)$ is convex

is $f = \Gamma$.

Consequence

Gamma's "naturalness" explains its repeated appearance in diverse scientific fields.

Physics: Hypersphere Volume

Volume of n-ball

$$V_n(R) = \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(\frac{n}{2} + 1)} R^n$$

Essential for statistical mechanics and high-dimensional geometry.

Physics: Veneziano Amplitude

String Scattering (1968)

Veneziano amplitude expressed via Beta:

$$A(s,t) \propto B(-\alpha(s), -\alpha(t))$$

Historic role in founding string theory.

Statistics: Gamma Distribution

Density

Shape $\alpha$, Rate $\beta$:

$$f(x) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta x}, \quad x > 0$$

Applications

  • Waiting times
  • Reliability Engineering
  • Insurance Claims

Statistics: Beta & Dirichlet

Beta Distribution

Models uncertainty on $[0,1]$.

Conjugate prior for Bernoulli/Binomial.

Dirichlet Distribution

Generalizes Beta to $K$ categories.

Used for LDA topic priors.

Matrix-Variate Generalization

Matrix Gamma

Multivariate Gamma $\Gamma_p(\cdot)$

Matrix analogues of scalar functions.

Application

Allow priors over covariance matrices (positive-definite matrices).

Wishart / Inverse-Wishart

Definitions

Wishart: Distribution over positive-definite matrices (covariance).

Inverse-Wishart: Common conjugate prior.

Notation

$$\mathcal{W}(V, \nu)$$

Used for learning covariance structure in data.

The AI Problem: "Confidently Wrong"

Standard deep nets output point estimates and can be overconfident on Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data.

We need models that quantify epistemic vs aleatoric uncertainty.

Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs)

Concept

BNNs place distributions over weights.

This allows uncertainty propagation through the model.

Matrix-Variate Priors for BNNs

Strategy

Use a Wishart / Matrix-Gamma prior on the covariance of weight matrices.

Result: The network learns correlations between weights.

Practical Payoff

Aleatoric Uncertainty

Data noise (Irreducible).

Epistemic Uncertainty

Model ignorance (Reducible).

Result

Enables the AI to say "I don't know."

Case Study 3: Medical Diagnosis

The Problem: "Black Box" AI

  • Standard AI is Overconfident.
  • Fails to measure Uncertainty.

Dangerous for high-stakes decisions.

The Solution: BNNs

Standard AI

Learns Single Point Estimates for weights.

Output: "This is Cancer (99%)."

Bayesian Neural Network

Learns Probability Distributions for weights.

Output: "This is likely Cancer, but uncertainty is High."

Matrix-variate priors (Wishart) allow learning of correlations.

Mahalanobis Distance

Definition

$$D_M(x) = \sqrt{(x-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (x-\mu)}$$

Accounts for covariance.

Better at detecting anomalous combinations than Euclidean distance.
